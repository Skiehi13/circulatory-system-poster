Comparative Analysis of Circulatory Systems Across Five Vertebrate Classes

A Veterinary Science Perspective on Cardiovascular Evolution and Function

LEOPARD SHARK

Triakis semifasciata

Class: Chondrichthyes

Figure 1A: Leopard shark (Triakis semifasciata) demonstrating typical benthic habitat behavior.

Figure 1B: Circulatory system showing single atrium, ventricle, and unidirectional blood flow.

Key Characteristics:

  • Single-loop circulatory system with unidirectional blood flow
  • Two-chambered heart: single atrium and single ventricle
  • Blood passes through gills for oxygenation before reaching body tissues
  • Lower blood pressure compared to mammals and birds
  • Conus arteriosus with valves prevents backflow into ventricle

ANDEAN COCK-OF-THE-ROCK

Rupicola peruvianus

Class: Aves

Figure 2A: Male Andean cock-of-the-rock displaying vibrant plumage for mating displays.

Figure 2B: Four-chambered avian heart with complete ventricular septation and right aortic arch.

Key Characteristics:

  • Four-chambered heart with complete separation of blood types
  • High metabolic rate supports flight and display behaviors
  • Right aortic arch (unlike mammals which have left aortic arch)
  • Large heart relative to body size for high oxygen demands
  • Rapid heart rate (up to 400+ bpm) for sustained flight

BLACK-FOOTED CAT

Felis nigripes

Class: Mammalia

Figure 3A: Black-footed cat in natural arid habitat showing compact body structure.

Figure 3B: Mammalian four-chambered heart with left aortic arch and coronary circulation.

Key Characteristics:

  • Four-chambered heart with complete double circulation
  • Left aortic arch develops (right aortic arch regresses)
  • High blood pressure for efficient oxygen delivery
  • Coronary circulation provides oxygen directly to heart muscle
  • Rapid metabolic rate supports active hunting behavior

SURINAM TOAD

Pipa pipa

Class: Amphibia

Figure 4A: Surinam toad showing flattened body adaptation for aquatic lifestyle.

Figure 4B: Three-chambered amphibian heart with partial ventricular septation and dual circulation.

Key Characteristics:

  • Three-chambered heart with two atria and single ventricle
  • Relies heavily on cutaneous (skin) respiration underwater
  • Muscular ridges in ventricle minimize blood mixing
  • Pulmocutaneous circulation serves both lungs and skin
  • Flexible respiratory system adapted to aquatic lifestyle

MATA MATA TURTLE

Chelus fimbriata

Class: Reptilia

Figure 5A: Mata mata turtle underwater showing camouflage adaptations and fringed neck.

Figure 5B: Reptilian heart with three chambers and cardiac shunting capability for diving.

Key Characteristics:

  • Three-chambered heart with cardiac shunting capability
  • Can bypass lungs when submerged through right-to-left shunting
  • Incomplete ventricular septum allows blood flow redirection
  • Adapted for long periods underwater without breathing
  • Muscular ridges help separate oxygenated and deoxygenated blood
